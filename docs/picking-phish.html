A field guide · April 2026

Picking Phish

A plain-language walk through the computer model we're teaching to guess what song Phish will play next — how it thinks, where it shines, and where it trips over its own feet.

v7 · shipped on NAS 2,250 shows studied · 983 songs
§ 01 · the question

What are we even doing?

Every time Phish takes the stage, they face a decision: of the nearly one thousand songs they know, which one do they play next? We're building a computer program that tries to answer that same question, one slot at a time.

You feed it the show so far — the venue, the date, what songs have already been played tonight, what set we're in. It thinks for a second, then hands back a ranked list of all the songs, with what it considers most likely at the top. If you're watching at home and it puts the actual next song in the top five, that's a good night.

It doesn't know music. It has never been to a show. It has read a lot of setlists. — what the model actually is

Thirty years of Phish setlists (every one on record) are the model's entire universe. Patterns it spots there are the only patterns it has. When it nails a prediction, it's because something about this moment looks like something that happened in 1998 or 2015, and the song that came next then tends to come next now.

§ 02 · why it's hard

Phish is a terrible band to predict.

Most bands have a setlist. Phish has a repertoire, a set of unwritten rules, and a pathological aversion to playing the same song twice in a single three-night stand.

~16slots per show
< 1%odds if you guessed random
3.6%what the model gets

The constraints that matter

Every show is a slot-by-slot decision. Set 1 opens with something energetic. Set 2 tends to get weirder, longer, more jam-oriented. Encores are short and loaded with meaning. Each of those slots behaves differently — a song that's a great set-1 opener is a strange choice for an encore.

The once-per-run norm. Over a multi-night stand, the band almost never repeats a song. So if they played Tweezer last night, you can essentially write it off tonight.

The segue vocabulary. Some song pairs are nearly sacred. Mike's Song → I Am Hydrogen → Weekapaug Groove is practically one piece of music. Colonel Forbin's Ascent almost always leads to Fly Famous Mockingbird. These "bigrams" — two-song sequences — are the model's most reliable signal.

Bustouts. Every show might contain a song the band hasn't played in 200+ shows, pulled out to delight a specific audience or mark an occasion. These are essentially unpredictable, and the band knows it.

§ 03 · how it thinks

Forty-seven things the model looks at.

At every slot, for every one of 983 candidate songs, the model computes a set of numbers — "features" — that describe the situation. It then scores each candidate by blending those numbers in a way it learned from thirty years of setlists. Here's the guided tour.

GROUP 01

How often they play it

The base rates. Total times this song has ever been played, times in the last year, whether it's a cover, what year it debuted.

  • total_plays_ever
  • plays_last_12mo
  • debut_year · is_cover
GROUP 02

When it was last played

Recency. How many shows ago, how many days ago. Separate clocks for the current tour and the current multi-night run.

  • shows_since_last_played
  • days_since_last_played
  • this tour · this run
GROUP 03

Venue & run context

Have they played this song at this venue before? How many nights into this run are we? How much run is left? Some songs are closers; some are debut-night staples.

  • times_at_venue
  • run_position · run_length
  • venue_debut_affinity
GROUP 04

Tour patterns

Within a given tour, some songs get rotated as openers or closers. The model tracks how often each song has opened or closed on this tour.

  • tour_opener_rate
  • tour_closer_rate
  • times_this_tour
GROUP 05

In-show context

What set are we in, what's already been played tonight, what song just ended, and did it end in a segue (→). The bigram feature — "how often does the previous song lead to this one" — lives here, and it's the single most powerful signal the model has.

  • current_set · set_position
  • prev_song_id · segue_mark
  • bigram_prev_to_this
  • opener/closer/encore rates
GROUP 06

Time & era

Day of the week, month, the Phish "era" (pre-hiatus / post-hiatus / 3.0 / etc.), and album-recency: was this song on their most recent studio album?

  • day_of_week · month · era
  • days_since_last_new_album
  • is_from_latest_album
GROUP 07

Derived "role" scores

Composite numbers that try to capture roles: is this a set-opener type? A jam vehicle? An encore finisher? A candidate for a bust-out?

  • opener_score · closer_score
  • encore_score
  • bustout_score
Under the hood

The model that chews through these numbers is called LightGBM — a standard modern ranking model. It's a forest of simple yes/no decision trees that vote on which song should be on top. If that phrase means nothing to you, it doesn't matter: what matters is that it learns which of the 47 signals above are worth paying attention to, and when.

Reading the scoreboard

Four of sixteen slots landed in the top five — that's a 25% "top-5 rate" on this particular night. The model's headline number across many nights is about 14% top-5, so this night was better than average. The standout Fly Famous Mockingbird top-1 hit is exactly where the model is strongest: when a well-worn segue tees up an obvious next move, it nails it.

The spectacular misses tell a more interesting story. Walk Away at slot 7 is a James Gang cover — and unbeknownst to the model, Joe Walsh was in the audience that night. That's not something in any setlist file. I Am the Walrus and Tweezer Reprise in the encore are almost comical: Reprise is literally designed to follow Tweezer, which had been played four slots earlier — the model's memory of that connection seems to dissipate when set and encore boundaries get in the way.

Asterisk on slot 7

No model could have predicted Walk Away. Joe Walsh — who sang it — was at the show. These are the surprises that make a Phish concert what it is, and no amount of setlist-reading will find them.

§ 05 · strengths & weaknesses

What's working, what's wobbling.

What it's good at

Classic segues. Mike's → Hydrogen, Forbin's → Mockingbird, Tweezer → Possum (in-set). If the previous song reliably leads somewhere, the model knows. This single feature does almost a third of the work.

Set-1 openers. Openers have their own logic — newer songs, high-energy staples. The model distinguishes "opener type" songs from "middle-of-set-2 jam vehicle" songs pretty reliably.

Separating encores. It almost always puts short, heavy, sing-along songs at the top during encore slots. Not perfect, but the kind of song is usually right.

Where it's struggling

Anywhere inside set 2. A detailed analysis showed the model has no slot-specific intelligence for middle-of-set-2 or set-2 closer slots — it just runs generic rules. Unsurprisingly, that's where it misses most.

Cross-boundary memory. The Tweezer Reprise case. If a song was played five slots ago, the "what just came before" feature doesn't know about it. The model needs a longer memory.

Bustouts and guests. Songs the band hasn't played in years, or surprise covers pegged to audience members. Fundamentally unpredictable from setlist history — honestly, the model probably shouldn't try.

§ 07 · what's cooking

Things we're trying next.

Each of these is a candidate experiment. The idea is to do one at a time — if we bundle three changes and the score moves, we don't know which one helped. One hypothesis, one training run, one answer.

Verdict in

Slot-type flags · big win

Two simple yes/no signals — "are we in set 2?" and "is this the first song of a new set?" — nearly doubled Top-1 (3.6% → 6.8%) and added 6.7 points to Top-5 (14.5% → 21.2%) versus v5. Tweezer Reprise, the diagnostic encore miss that was at #109 under v6, climbed to #7. The slot-type flag is_first_in_set jumped to the second-most-influential feature in the model, just below the bigram.

delta vs v5: +3.2pp Top-1 · +6.7pp Top-5 · +43% MRR
artifact: v7 · ready to ship to NAS
Proposed

Longer bigram memory

Currently only the immediately-previous song influences the model's bigram feature. For every song already played tonight, we could check whether it historically leads to each candidate, and take the strongest match. Directly addresses the Tweezer Reprise miss.

target: encore & late-show prediction
status: design phase
Proposed

Cleanup: dead features

Six features are currently contributing nothing — the model never consults them. Probably some plumbing bugs (role scores stuck at zero) and some genuinely weak signals. Cleaning these out shouldn't change performance but makes the model easier to understand.

targets: opener_score, encore_score,
middle_of_set_2_score, segue_mark_in,
set2_opener_rate, historical_gap_mean
Proposed

Cover-song handling

Covers like Walk Away and I Am the Walrus get systematically under-ranked. The model penalizes them as a class, but covers are often the most "special night" picks. A nuanced treatment might help — though the honest answer is that many cover appearances are triggered by external events we can't see.

from setlist data alone
Investigation

Slot-conditional importance

Rather than asking "which features matter most overall?", ask "which features matter for set-1 openers versus set-2 middles versus encores?" We already ran this; it revealed the blind spots above. Worth rerunning once v7 ships to see if the slot-type flags fix the gaps.

evidence-generating tool
not a model change itself
Deferred

Nightly smoke test

Every morning, automatically take last night's actual setlist, ask the model what it would have predicted at each slot, and log the ranks. Builds up an honest, live track record of the deployed model's real-world accuracy — independent of the offline evaluation metrics.

§ 08 · glossary

Words worth knowing.

Feature
A single number that describes some aspect of the situation — "how many shows ago was this song last played," for example. The model works with 47 of them.
Bigram
A pair of songs played back-to-back. The most important single signal in the model.
Top-1 / Top-5
The share of slots where the real answer was the model's #1 pick (Top-1) or within its first five picks (Top-5). Higher is better.
MRR
Mean Reciprocal Rank. A single number: if the real answer is ranked 2nd, the score for that slot is ½; if ranked 4th, ¼. Averaged across all slots. Rewards putting the right answer near the top, penalizes burying it.
Walk-forward evaluation
To check how good the model is without cheating: hide the last 20 shows, re-train on everything before them, then test on each hidden show. Mimics what the model sees in real time.
Slot
One position in a setlist. Slot 1 is the opener, slot 9 is (often) the set-2 opener, slot 15 or 16 is (usually) the encore.
Once-per-run
Phish's informal rule: across a multi-night stand at one venue, they try not to repeat a song. Heavily encoded in the model's recency features.
